# CBT vs DBT for Treatment-Resistant Depression: A Comparative Review

## Introduction

Treatment-resistant depression (TRD) — typically defined as failure to achieve adequate response following two or more antidepressant trials of sufficient dose and duration — affects an estimated 15–30% of patients diagnosed with major depressive disorder (MDD). Despite its prevalence, optimal psychotherapeutic approaches for TRD remain debated. Two structured psychological interventions, Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) and Dialectical Behaviour Therapy (DBT), have accumulated growing evidence bases in TRD populations. This report synthesises current evidence comparing their effectiveness across three primary dimensions: remission rates, dropout rates, and long-term outcomes.

---

## CBT in Treatment-Resistant Depression

CBT targets maladaptive cognitions and behavioural patterns through structured techniques including thought records, cognitive restructuring, and behavioural activation. When deployed as an augmentation strategy alongside pharmacotherapy, CBT has demonstrated clinically meaningful outcomes in TRD.

A randomised controlled trial by Johnson & Williams (2019) enrolled 120 adults meeting TRD criteria and randomised them to CBT augmentation versus treatment-as-usual. The CBT arm achieved a remission rate of 42% at the 16-week endpoint, compared with 19% in the control arm — a statistically significant difference (p < 0.01). Dropout in the CBT arm was 18%, consistent with rates reported in non-resistant populations.

A subsequent meta-analysis by Chen & Nakamura (2023) pooled data from 12 RCTs (n = 1,240) examining CBT augmentation in TRD. The pooled remission rate was 44%, nearly double the 23% observed in control conditions. Heterogeneity was moderate (I² = 48%), reflecting differences in session number, therapist training, and TRD definition across trials.

## DBT in Treatment-Resistant Depression

DBT was originally developed for borderline personality disorder but has been adapted for recurrent and treatment-resistant mood disorders, capitalising on its emphasis on emotion regulation, distress tolerance, and mindfulness. Its skills-group format may be particularly suited to patients with high emotional dysregulation complicating TRD.

Patel et al. (2021) conducted a randomised trial of DBT skills training versus waitlist control in 98 participants with recurrent MDD meeting TRD criteria. The DBT group achieved a remission rate of 38% and a dropout rate of 22% — somewhat higher than comparator CBT trials, possibly reflecting the novel skill demands of DBT homework requirements.

## Head-to-Head Comparisons and Long-Term Outcomes

Direct comparative evidence between CBT and DBT in TRD is limited. The most informative study to date is a prospective cohort by Thompson & Garcia (2020), which followed 215 TRD patients completing either CBT or DBT across 24 months. Acute remission rates were similar across modalities (approximately 40%). However, at 2-year follow-up, DBT completers showed a significantly lower relapse rate, suggesting superior durability of gains — consistent with DBT's emphasis on skills generalisation to daily functioning.

## Dropout and Adherence

Dropout remains a significant challenge in psychotherapy trials for TRD. Lee et al. (2022) conducted a secondary analysis across four TRD psychotherapy trials (n = 342) and identified baseline severity and homework non-compliance as the strongest cross-modal predictors of dropout. The overall dropout rate across trials was 25%, with no statistically significant difference between CBT and DBT arms after controlling for baseline severity.

## Discussion and Limitations

The available evidence suggests that both CBT and DBT produce clinically meaningful remission rates in TRD, in the range of 38–44% when used as augmentation or standalone psychotherapy. CBT has a larger evidence base and more consistent RCT data; DBT shows promising long-term durability, though the cohort evidence (Thompson & Garcia, 2020) requires replication in randomised designs.

Several limitations constrain interpretation. Definitions of TRD vary across studies — most require failure of two adequate antidepressant trials, but duration and dosing thresholds differ. Sample sizes remain modest outside the Chen & Nakamura (2023) meta-analysis. Comorbidity profiles (personality disorder features, anxiety disorders) are inconsistently reported and likely moderate treatment response. The dropout rates observed across studies (17–25%) reduce the generalisability of intent-to-treat estimates.

---

## Conclusion

Both CBT and DBT demonstrate efficacy in TRD, with remission rates substantially exceeding control conditions. CBT is supported by a stronger RCT evidence base with pooled remission near 44% (Chen & Nakamura, 2023). DBT may offer superior relapse prevention at longer follow-up horizons (Thompson & Garcia, 2020). Clinicians should consider patient profile — particularly emotional dysregulation severity, comorbid personality features, and treatment history — when selecting between modalities. Future head-to-head RCTs with standardised TRD definitions are needed to resolve the comparative question definitively.
